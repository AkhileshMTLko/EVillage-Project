History Of The Village:-

"The soul of India lives in its villages," declared Mahatma Gandhi[12] at the beginning of the 20th
century. According to the 2011 census of India, 69% of Indians (around 833 million people) live in
villages.[13] As per the 2011 census of India, there are a total of 649,481 villages in India [14].The size
of these villages varies considerably. 236,004 Indian villages have a population of fewer than 500,
while 3,976 villages have a population of 10,000+. Most of the villages have their own temple,
mosque, or church, depending on the local religious following.

A village is a clustered human settlement or community, larger than a hamlet but smaller
than a town (although the word is often used to describe all human settlements without
municipal rights,[1] including smaller towns and hamlets; sometimes even defined as a
settlement having a church, unlike a hamlet, but no market),[2][3][4][5] with a
population typically ranging from a few hundred to a few thousand.

Freedom Fighter Of The Village:-

The village has a proud legacy of brave souls who played significant roles in India's struggle for independence.
The spirit of patriotism was deeply ingrained in the community, as several villagers stepped forward to
contribute to the nation’s fight against British colonial rule.


Lal Bahadur Shastri :-

Jai Jawan Jai Kisan (English: "Hail the Soldier, Hail the Farmer") was a slogan of Lal Bahadur Shastri,
was an influential leader who fought in various movements like the Quit India Movement. His commitment to non-violent protests and his resilience under British rule earned him respect not only in the village but across the region. He was imprisoned several times but remained steadfast in his belief in an independent India.


The Legacy:-

The sacrifices made by the village's freedom fighters are a source of immense pride for its people. Their stories of resistance and courage are passed down through generations, ensuring that the flame of patriotism burns bright in the hearts of the villagers. Memorials and annual ceremonies held in the village are reminders of their contribution, and the community continues to honor their legacy with great respect.

The Festival: Chhath Puja :-

Chhath Puja is an ancient Hindu festival dedicated to the Sun God, Surya, and his consort, Usha (the goddess of dawn). Celebrated primarily in the states of Bihar, Jharkhand, Uttar Pradesh, and Nepal, this festival holds deep cultural and spiritual significance. Devotees offer prayers to the setting and rising sun, expressing gratitude for sustaining life on earth
During Chhath Puja, women, known as ‘Vratis,’ observe a strict fast and perform various rituals to seek blessings for their family’s happiness and success.
Fasting during Chhath Puja is considered extremely strict, as it includes abstaining from food and water for almost 36 hours.The main objective of Chhath Puja is to show gratitude to the Sun God, who is considered the source of all energy and life on Earth.The rituals of Chhath Puja include fasting, offering prayers to the rising and setting sun, and taking holy dips in rivers or ponds.
